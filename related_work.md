## 7. Related Work

**Row buffer management with static policies and address mapping.**
The open-page and close-page tradeoff and the limitations of existing adaptive schemes are discussed in Section 2. Quantitative comparisons with ABP, DYMPL, and INTAP are presented in Section 5. JEDEC standards define a per-command auto-precharge mechanism that allows the memory controller to attach a precharge directive to individual read or write commands, upon which all adaptive row buffer management schemes are built. Kaseridis et al. [Kaseridis+11] proposed Minimalist Open-page, which bounds the number of row buffer hits per activation through an address mapping that distributes column address bits across the bank address field. This design mitigates row buffer monopolization while preserving partial spatial locality, though the fixed hit limit is determined at design time and does not adapt across workloads or execution phases. Mi et al. [Mi+09] proposed PARBLO, a software-based approach that controls operating system page allocation order to improve data placement in DRAM and reduce row buffer conflicts. PARBLO is complementary to hardware-based page policy mechanisms, though its applicability is restricted to programs with regular, affine loop structures. Unlike static policies or fixed address mappings, CRAFT continuously adjusts the idle timeout per bank, enabling it to track workload phase changes and inter-bank locality differences at runtime.

**Predictor-based row buffer management.**
A fundamental challenge shared by predictor-based approaches is that memory requests have already been filtered through multiple cache levels before reaching the memory controller, causing much of the original access pattern structure to be lost. Park and Park [Park+03] first applied two-bit saturating counters, adapted from branch prediction, to page policy decisions. Their per-bank variant requires only four counters, while the per-row variant demands storage that scales with memory capacity. Stankovic and Milenkovic [Stankovic+05] extended this line of work with a cascaded predictor combining a zero-live-time detector with a dead-time predictor. Their observation that premature closure incurs a higher penalty than delayed closure anticipates the cost-asymmetric framework that CRAFT formalizes. Xu et al. [Xu+09] adapted two-level branch prediction to page policy by maintaining a history register and a pattern history table with two-bit saturating counters. They explored per-global, per-bank, and per-page granularities for both the history register and the pattern history table. The per-page variants achieve the highest prediction accuracy but require storage that grows linearly with the number of tracked pages.

Awasthi et al. [Awasthi+11] proposed ABP, which tracks per-row access counts in a 2048-set, four-way set-associative history table of approximately 20 KB per channel. ABP predicts the total number of accesses a row will receive and closes the row buffer after the predicted count is reached. The predictor lookup latency is hidden behind normal DRAM access latency, but the storage requirement presents scalability challenges as the number of banks per channel increases. Shen et al. [Shen+14] proposed RBPP, which maintains a small set of per-bank registers storing the addresses of frequently accessed rows. If the currently active row matches one of these registered entries, the row is kept open; otherwise it is closed immediately. RBPP reduces storage overhead by 91% compared to ABP, though its binary open-or-close decision operates at a coarser granularity than schemes that adjust a continuous timeout value. Ghasempour et al. [Ghasempour+16] proposed HAPPY, which replaces per-row tracking state with per-address-bit counters, reducing storage scaling from linear in the number of rows to logarithmic. HAPPY achieves prediction accuracy comparable to per-row approaches while requiring orders of magnitude less storage. As a state compression technique, HAPPY is orthogonal to the underlying feedback mechanism and could potentially be combined with CRAFT's cost-asymmetric adaptation. Unlike these prediction-based schemes, CRAFT treats precharge outcomes as direct feedback rather than predicting future accesses from historical patterns, avoiding both the storage overhead of per-row tracking and the accuracy limitations inherent in prediction after cache filtering.

**Timeout-based adaptive schemes.**
Timeout-based approaches precharge a row after a configurable idle period. By adjusting the timeout value, the policy spans the full range from open-page to close-page behavior. Ghasempour et al. [Ghasemp+16] proposed INTAP, a per-bank mechanism that adjusts the timeout using a mistake counter with a fixed step size of 50 cycles. INTAP treats wrong precharges and conflicts symmetrically, applying the same adjustment magnitude regardless of their differing latency costs. The fixed step size further constrains adaptation speed, requiring up to 63 consecutive adjustments in the same direction to traverse the full timeout range. Srikanth et al. [Srikanth+18] proposed a two-layer design that extends the timeout-based approach. The first layer maintains a per-bank scoreboard that projects row buffer hit and conflict counts across seven candidate timeout values and selects the value maximizing the net increase in hits over conflicts. The second layer implements a row exclusion mechanism that identifies high-locality rows and exempts them from timeout-based closure. The combined storage overhead is approximately 1.1 KB per channel. CRAFT shares the timeout adjustment paradigm but differs in its feedback mechanism. Rather than projecting outcomes across multiple candidate windows or applying uniform step sizes, CRAFT derives asymmetric adjustment steps from the cost difference between wrong precharges and conflicts. Exponential backoff enables rapid convergence during high-locality phases, while cost-proportional de-escalation provides conservative timeout reduction. This mechanism achieves finer-grained adaptation at 140 B per channel, approximately 8× smaller than the scoreboard-based design of Srikanth et al. [Srikanth+18].

**Learning-based approaches.**
Ipek et al. [Ipek+08] modeled DRAM command scheduling as an infinite-horizon discounted Markov decision process and applied SARSA reinforcement learning with CMAC function approximation. The approach requires 32 KB of SRAM for function approximation tables and a five-stage pipelined learning engine operating at processor frequency. While the learned policy achieves a 19% average IPC improvement over FR-FCFS, the hardware complexity and lack of interpretability present practical deployment challenges. Rafique and Zhu proposed three progressively refined approaches to row buffer management. FAPS-3D [Rafique+19] deploys per-bank two-bit saturating counter state machines within 3D-stacked DRAM vault controllers to classify banks into four hit-rate tiers and switch between open-page and close-page policies accordingly. The mechanism requires minimal overhead but is limited to binary policy decisions per bank and does not support intermediate timeout values. Rafique and Zhu [Rafique+21] subsequently combined dynamic page mode management with conflict-aware memory-side prefetching in 3D-stacked DRAM, maintaining a row utilization table and a conflict table to identify prefetch candidates with a total metadata overhead of approximately 4.9 KB. DYMPL [Rafique+22] further advanced this line of work by modeling page policy as a binary classification problem using perceptron learning with seven features, including page utilization, hotness, recency, and column stride. Each prediction requires seven table lookups followed by six additions on the critical path. Furthermore, 86.6% of the 3.39 KB per channel storage budget is consumed by a 512-entry page record table. These learning-based approaches rely on indirect features that are themselves estimates of the underlying access patterns, inherently introducing estimation error and training overhead. CRAFT observes that the three precharge outcomes already encode sufficient feedback for effective adaptation, without requiring feature engineering, model training, or associative table lookups.

**Application-aware and cross-layer techniques.**
Xie et al. [Xie+13] combined bank partitioning with application-aware page policy assignment, profiling per-application memory access intensity and row buffer hit rate at runtime to assign open-page or close-page policy at the granularity of application-bank pairs. This approach requires bank-level isolation across applications, which constrains memory address mapping flexibility. Rixner et al. [Rixner+00] introduced FR-FCFS scheduling, which reorders pending memory requests to prioritize row buffer hits. FR-FCFS addresses request ordering rather than the page policy decision and is orthogonal to CRAFT. Lee et al. [Lee+08] proposed prefetch-aware DRAM scheduling, which adaptively adjusts the scheduling priority of prefetch requests based on per-core prefetch accuracy measurements. Prefetch-aware scheduling is complementary to CRAFT's per-bank timeout mechanism, as the two operate on different dimensions of memory controller design.

**DRAM architectural modifications.**
Several works have addressed row buffer performance through modifications to the DRAM device itself. Subramanian et al. [Subramanian+18b] introduced isolation and equalization transistors that decouple the sense amplifier from the bitline, enabling simultaneous read and precharge operations and reducing effective tRCD by 25%. Rohbani et al. [Rohbani+21] proposed PF-DRAM, which eliminates the precharge phase entirely through an alternative bitline sensing circuit that handles all possible initial bitline voltage states without requiring a conventional precharge step. Gulur et al. [Gulur+12] proposed replacing the single monolithic row buffer with multiple smaller sub-row buffers to capture temporal locality across multiple rows simultaneously, enabling early precharge scheduling and intra-bank parallelism. These circuit-level techniques require changes to the DRAM chip, whereas CRAFT operates entirely at the memory controller level within existing DRAM standards. The two categories of approach are orthogonal, and CRAFT's adaptive timeout mechanism can be combined with these architectural modifications for compounded latency and energy improvements.
