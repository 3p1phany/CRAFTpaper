## 7. Related Work

**Row buffer management policies.** We discuss the open-page and closed-page tradeoff and the limitations of existing adaptive schemes in Section 2, and present quantitative comparisons with ABP, DYMPL, and INTAP in Section 5. JEDEC standards [JEDEC] support per-command auto-precharge, but the decision remains static and lacks runtime adaptivity. Kaseridis et al. [Kaseridis+11] proposed Minimalist Open-page, which limits page hits per activation through address mapping that distributes column bits across the bank address. The approach bounds row buffer starvation while preserving partial locality, though the fixed hit limit does not adapt across workloads. Unlike static policies, CRAFT continuously adjusts idle timeout per bank to operate at the optimal point along the full policy spectrum.

**Predictor-based row buffer management.** A fundamental challenge shared by predictor-based approaches is that memory requests have already been filtered through multiple cache levels before reaching the memory controller, causing much of the original access pattern semantics to be lost. Park and Park [Park+03] first applied 2-bit saturating counters to page policy. Stankovic and Milenkovic [Stankovic+05] extended this with a cascaded predictor that combines a zero-live-time detector with a dead-time predictor. Their observation that premature closure incurs higher cost than delayed closure anticipates the cost-asymmetric framework that CRAFT formalizes. Xu et al. [Xu+09] adapted two-level branch prediction to page policy, though the per-page variants require substantial storage.

Awasthi et al. [Awasthi+11] proposed ABP, which tracks per-row access counts in a history table of approximately 20 KB per channel. ABP predicts the number of accesses a row will receive before closing, with the predictor lookup hidden behind normal DRAM access latency. The storage requirement presents scalability challenges as the number of banks per channel increases. Shen et al. [Shen+14] proposed RBPP, which maintains four per-bank registers for frequently accessed rows and achieves performance comparable to ABP with less storage, though it operates at a coarser granularity. Unlike these prediction-based schemes, CRAFT treats precharge outcomes as direct feedback rather than predicting future accesses from historical patterns, avoiding both the storage overhead of per-row tracking and the accuracy limitations inherent in prediction.

**Timeout-based adaptive schemes.** Timeout-based approaches precharge a row after a configurable idle period. By adjusting the timeout value, the policy spans the full range from open-page to closed-page behavior. Ghasempour et al. [Ghasemp+16] proposed INTAP, a per-bank mechanism that adjusts timeout using a mistake counter with a fixed step size. INTAP treats wrong precharges and conflicts symmetrically despite their differing latency costs. The fixed step size further limits the rate of adaptation during workload phase transitions. Srikanth et al. [Srikanth+18] extended the timeout-based approach with a global scoreboard that projects hit and conflict counts across multiple candidate timeout windows per bank. The scoreboard selects the timeout that maximizes the net increase in hits over conflicts. Their design also incorporates a row exclusion mechanism that identifies high-locality rows and exempts them from timeout-based closure. CRAFT shares the timeout adjustment paradigm but differs fundamentally in its feedback mechanism. Rather than projecting outcomes across multiple windows or using symmetric step sizes, CRAFT derives asymmetric steps from the cost difference between wrong precharges and conflicts. Exponential backoff enables rapid convergence during high-locality phases, while cost-proportional de-escalation provides conservative timeout reduction. This dual mechanism achieves finer-grained adaptation at only 140 B per channel.

**Learning-based approaches.** Ipek et al. [Ipek+08] modeled DRAM scheduling as a Markov decision process and applied reinforcement learning with CMAC function approximation to learn policies that include implicit precharge decisions. The approach requires substantial SRAM and a pipelined learning engine operating at CPU frequency, and the learned policy lacks interpretability. Rafique and Zhu proposed two increasingly sophisticated approaches. FAPS-3D [Rafique+19] uses per-bank finite state machines to switch between open-page and closed-page in 3D-stacked DRAM, though it is limited to binary decisions per bank. DYMPL [Rafique+22] models page policy as a binary classification problem using perceptron learning with seven features. The feature lookup adds latency to the critical path. In addition, the training signal formulation introduces bias toward close-page predictions. These learning-based approaches rely on indirect features, inherently introducing estimation error and training overhead. CRAFT observes that the three precharge outcomes already encode the necessary feedback directly, without requiring feature engineering or model training.

**DRAM architectural modifications.** Several works address row buffer performance through modifications to the DRAM device itself [Subramanian+18b, Rohbani+21, Gulur+12]. Subramanian et al. [Subramanian+18b] introduced isolation and equalization transistors to enable simultaneous read and precharge. Rohbani et al. [Rohbani+21] proposed eliminating precharge entirely through alternative bitline sensing. Gulur et al. [Gulur+12] proposed multiple sub-row buffers to capture temporal locality across multiple rows simultaneously. These circuit-level techniques require changes to the DRAM chip, whereas CRAFT operates entirely at the memory controller level within existing DRAM standards. The two approaches are orthogonal and could be combined for further latency reduction.

**Other related approaches.** Rixner et al. [Rixner+00] introduced FR-FCFS scheduling, which prioritizes row-buffer-hit requests within the memory access scheduler. FR-FCFS addresses request ordering rather than the page policy decision and is orthogonal to CRAFT. Ghasempour et al. [Ghasempour+16] proposed HAPPY, which replaces per-row tracking with per-address-bit tracking to reduce prediction state storage. As a storage compression technique, HAPPY is orthogonal to CRAFT and could potentially be combined with its feedback mechanism. Other cross-layer techniques, including application-aware page policy with bank partitioning [Xie+13], software-directed page allocation for row buffer locality [Mi+09], and prefetch-aware DRAM scheduling [Lee+08], are complementary to CRAFT's per-bank adaptive timeout mechanism.

In summary, prior row buffer management schemes either remain static or achieve adaptivity at the expense of substantial storage or computational overhead. CRAFT resolves this tradeoff through cost-asymmetric feedback that requires only 140 B per channel. Furthermore, CRAFT's controller-level operation is orthogonal to DRAM architectural improvements, scheduling policies, and cross-layer optimization techniques, and can compound the benefits of such complementary approaches.
