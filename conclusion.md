## 8. Conclusion

This paper presents CRAFT, a lightweight feedback-driven row buffer management scheme that exploits the inherent cost asymmetry among precharge outcomes. The central observation is that the three possible outcomes of a speculative timeout precharge—right, wrong, and conflict—encode fundamentally different performance penalties, and this cost structure can directly govern both the direction and magnitude of timeout adjustments without requiring prediction tables or learned models.

CRAFT implements this principle through a per-bank feedback loop that combines cost-asymmetric step sizes with exponential backoff for rapid convergence during high-locality phases, operating over a continuous timeout range. Three precharge-path refinements—right streak de-escalation, read/write cost differentiation, and streak decay—further improve adaptation precision by leveraging information already available within the precharge outcome classification. An ablation study confirms that the core feedback loop alone accounts for 76% of the total performance gain, and that augmenting the loop with conflict-path signals degrades rather than improves performance. This finding validates the design principle that the three-way precharge outcome classification encodes sufficient feedback information for effective timeout adaptation.

Across 12 memory-intensive benchmarks spanning graph traversal, graph analysis, and scientific computing workloads, CRAFT achieves geometric mean IPC improvements of 7.73%, 3.10%, and 2.84% over ABP, DYMPL, and INTAP, respectively. These gains are attributable to a 5.62 percentage point average improvement in read row buffer hit rate, which translates to a 2.74% reduction in average read latency. CRAFT requires only 140 B of storage per channel—35 bits per bank with no specialized hardware structures—representing a 24.8x reduction compared to DYMPL and a 30.3x reduction compared to RL_PAGE, while delivering superior performance. The per-bank state organization, absence of shared data structures, and minimal critical-path computation (one comparison and one addition) make CRAFT readily integrable into existing memory controller designs.
