\begin{abstract}
DRAM row buffer management presents a fundamental tradeoff between exploiting row buffer locality and minimizing conflict penalties. The optimal policy varies across workloads, execution phases, and individual address ranges. Existing adaptive schemes, however, face a persistent tension between hardware complexity and management effectiveness.

This paper presents CRAFT, a lightweight, cost-aware adaptive timeout scheme for DRAM row buffer management. CRAFT exploits the observation that the two failure modes of speculative precharge incur qualitatively different performance costs. It translates this cost asymmetry into differentiated per-bank timeout adjustments. Incorrect speculative precharges increase the timeout through exponential backoff, whereas conflicts reduce it with a smaller step proportional to the avoidable overhead. Beyond this core feedback loop, three precharge-outcome-based refinements further improve adaptation precision without additional hardware structures. The entire mechanism requires no prediction tables, feature extraction, or learned models.

We evaluate CRAFT using a cycle-accurate simulator across 12 memory-intensive workloads from graph analytics and scientific computing. CRAFT improves IPC by 7.73\%, 3.10\%, and 2.84\% over ABP, DYMPL, and INTAP, respectively. The total storage overhead is 140~bytes per channel, representing a 24$\times$ reduction relative to DYMPL and more than a 140$\times$ reduction relative to ABP. These results indicate that the cost asymmetry inherent in precharge outcomes is an effective basis for lightweight row buffer management in our evaluated setting.

\section{Introduction}

Main memory latency remains a primary performance bottleneck in modern processor systems~\cite{Wulf95,Mutlu13,Hennessy19}.
A critical factor governing this latency is how the memory controller manages the \emph{row buffer} in each DRAM bank, the internal sense-amplifier array that caches the most recently activated row.
Holding a row open (the \emph{open-page} policy) maximizes the chance of serving subsequent accesses to the same row at low latency, but risks expensive conflicts when a subsequent request targets a different row. Closing immediately after each access (the \emph{close-page} policy) eliminates such conflicts but forfeits all potential row locality.
This fundamental tradeoff makes row buffer management one of the most extensively studied yet persistently challenging aspects of memory controller design~\cite{Zhang00,Jacob07}.

The optimal page policy varies across workloads, across execution phases, and even across targeted address ranges.
Neither open-page nor close-page dominates across all workloads. Open-page loses up to 11\% IPC on workloads with frequent row buffer conflicts. Close-page sacrifices up to 28\% IPC on workloads with high row buffer locality (Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation}(a)).
Within a single program execution, the row buffer hit rate shifts dramatically across program phases. The row buffer hit rate for \texttt{mcf} transitions from above 90\% to below 10\%, while \texttt{PageRank} follows the inverse trajectory (Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation}(b)).
Beyond temporal variation, row buffer locality also differs substantially across banks within a single channel. Under the open-page policy, \texttt{omnetpp} exhibits a per-bank row buffer hit rate coefficient of variation of 1.51, indicating pronounced inter-bank disparity.
These observations suggest that effective row buffer management should adapt per bank and per execution phase.

\label{fig:motivation}

Achieving per-bank, phase-adaptive row buffer management with low hardware overhead remains an open problem. Prior adaptive schemes have addressed aspects of this challenge but face a fundamental tradeoff between hardware complexity and adaptation precision.
ABP~\cite{Awasthi11} requires approximately 20~KB per channel for per-row prediction tables. DYMPL~\cite{Rafique22} employs a perceptron-based classifier with 3.39~KB per channel and requires machine-learning inference on the critical path. INTAP~\cite{Intel06} adjusts a per-bank timeout using approximately 200~bytes per channel. It applies the same fixed step size to both incorrect speculative precharges and conflicts. This symmetric adjustment does not distinguish between the penalty induced by speculative precharge decisions and the inherent cost of the workload's access pattern.

This paper presents CRAFT, a lightweight, cost-aware adaptive timeout scheme for DRAM row buffer management. Among the four observable outcomes of timeout-based speculative precharge, CRAFT focuses on three feedback-relevant cases: correct speculative precharge, incorrect speculative precharge, and conflict. It translates their asymmetric performance costs into differentiated per-bank timeout adjustments. Incorrect speculative precharges increase the timeout through exponential backoff, whereas conflicts reduce it with a smaller proportional step. The mechanism requires no prediction tables, feature extraction, or learned models. Section~\ref{sec:core} presents the full cost analysis and design rationale.

The contributions of this paper are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item We show that incorrect speculative precharges and conflicts incur qualitatively different performance costs, and we propose CRAFT, a feedback-driven scheme that translates this cost asymmetry into differentiated per-bank timeout adjustments while requiring only 140~bytes per channel, representing more than a 140$\times$ storage reduction compared to ABP.
\item Through ablation analysis, we show that precharge outcomes provide an effective basis for timeout adaptation in our evaluated setting, without requiring auxiliary runtime metrics.
\item We evaluate CRAFT on 12 memory-intensive workloads under a DDR5-4800 configuration. CRAFT improves IPC relative to ABP, DYMPL, and INTAP across the evaluated workloads.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{DRAM Row Buffer Fundamentals}

Modern DRAM organizes each bank around a \emph{row buffer}, an array of sense amplifiers that latches the most recently activated row. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:timing}(a), access latency depends on the row buffer state: a hit incurs only $t_\mathrm{CAS}$, a miss requires $t_\mathrm{RCD} + t_\mathrm{CAS}$, and a conflict requires precharge followed by activation, totaling $t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD} + t_\mathrm{CAS}$. An \emph{open-page} policy keeps the row buffer open to exploit hits; a \emph{close-page} policy precharges immediately to avoid conflicts. Neither static policy dominates universally, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation}.

\paragraph{Timeout-based speculative precharge.}
Timeout-based policies occupy a middle ground between open-page and close-page behavior. The memory controller starts a countdown timer when a row becomes idle. If the timer expires before the next request, the controller speculatively precharges the bank. A short timeout approximates close-page behavior. A long timeout approximates open-page behavior. This speculation produces one of four outcomes, illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:timing}(b):

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Correct speculative precharge.} The next request targets a different row; the speculative precharge correctly anticipated the end of locality. The effective cost is zero.
\item \textbf{Incorrect speculative precharge.} The next request targets the same row. The controller must reactivate the row. Both the precharge and the reactivation are entirely unnecessary operations that would not occur under the open-page baseline.
\item \textbf{Conflict.} The next request targets a different row, but the timeout has not yet expired. The controller issues an on-demand precharge and then activates the target row. The activation cost is inherent to the access pattern. Only the opportunity to overlap precharge with idle bank cycles is lost.
\item \textbf{Row buffer hit.} The next request targets the same row and arrives before the timeout, incurring zero additional cost. Because no precharge is issued in this case, CRAFT's feedback loop operates on the first three outcomes only.
\end{enumerate}

A closed-bank access requires $t_\mathrm{RCD} + t_\mathrm{CAS}$. A row buffer conflict pays $t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD} + t_\mathrm{CAS}$. The key challenge is to set the timeout value adaptively, per bank and over time, to match the evolving access pattern.

ABP~\cite{Awasthi11} requires approximately 20~KB per channel for a set-associative per-row prediction table, which is impractical for DDR5 controllers with 32 banks per channel. DYMPL~\cite{Rafique22} employs a perceptron over seven features with a 512-entry page row table (3.39~KB per channel); it does not directly observe the cost consequences of precharge decisions. INTAP~\cite{Intel06} adjusts a per-bank timeout through a mistake counter, but applies symmetric fixed-step adjustments that treat incorrect speculative precharges and conflicts identically.

These scheme-specific limitations differ in nature, but all three baselines share a deeper deficiency. None of them distinguishes precharge outcomes by the nature of the waste they produce. This distinction directly motivates CRAFT's cost-aware timeout adaptation, analyzed in detail in Section~\ref{sec:core}.

\section{CRAFT Design}

CRAFT exploits the cost asymmetry of precharge outcomes to adaptively manage DRAM row buffers. This section describes the core feedback loop (Section~\ref{sec:core}), three precharge-outcome-based refinements (Section~\ref{sec:refinements}), and hardware implementation (Section~\ref{sec:hardware}).

\subsection{Core Feedback Loop}
\label{sec:core}

CRAFT maintains a per-bank timeout counter that specifies the number of idle cycles after the last column access before the memory controller speculatively issues a precharge command.
CRAFT detects the three precharge outcomes at two distinct points.
A conflict occurs when a new request for a different row arrives while the timeout has not yet expired, indicating the timeout is too long.
Incorrect and correct speculative precharge outcomes are identified at the bank activation following a timeout-triggered precharge.
A same-row activation indicates an incorrect speculative precharge and signals that the timeout was too short.
A different-row activation indicates a correct speculative precharge and suggests that the current timeout value is appropriate.
These three outcomes provide a direct feedback signal for timeout adaptation.
CRAFT exploits the different performance costs of these outcomes to derive both the direction and the magnitude of each adjustment.

CRAFT adjusts the per-bank timeout value in response to these outcomes according to three design principles.

\paragraph{(a) Cost-driven asymmetric step sizes.}
Timeout-based speculative precharge serves both sides of the row buffer management trade-off. Keeping a row open allows same-row accesses to be served as row buffer hits. Issuing a timely precharge overlaps precharge overhead with idle bank cycles, reducing the effective penalty for subsequent different-row accesses. An incorrect speculative precharge eliminates the first benefit entirely. Both the precharge command and the subsequent reactivation are unnecessary operations that would not occur under the open-page baseline. The total controller-attributable waste therefore amounts to $t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD}$.

Conflicts present a fundamentally different cost model. The target row differs from the buffered row, so the precharge and subsequent activation are inherent to the access pattern regardless of the timeout decision. Only the timing constitutes a controller-attributable error. A correctly timed precharge would have overlapped $t_\mathrm{RP}$ with idle bank cycles. The avoidable overhead is therefore limited to the missed overlap opportunity of $t_\mathrm{RP}$.

The ratio of avoidable overhead between these two outcomes is $(t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD}) / t_\mathrm{RP}$. Under our DDR5-4800 configuration with $t_\mathrm{RP} = t_\mathrm{RCD} = 40$~cycles, the ratio is $2{:}1$. CRAFT mirrors this asymmetry in its adjustment magnitudes. The increment for incorrect speculative precharges uses a base value of \texttt{BASE\_STEP}~$= 50$~cycles, slightly larger than $t_\mathrm{RP}$, so each increase produces an observable change in the timeout. The decrement for conflicts is scaled by the factor $t_\mathrm{RP} / (t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD})$ and evaluates to 25~cycles under the same configuration.

This asymmetry creates a deliberate upward bias: under equal frequencies of incorrect speculative precharges and conflicts, net timeout increases accumulate, reflecting the $2{:}1$ cost ratio. $T_\mathrm{MAX}$ bounds this bias at 3200~cycles; conflicts and timeout reduction after consecutive correct speculative precharges then provide downward pressure.

\paragraph{(b) Exponential backoff.}
CRAFT applies exponential backoff to consecutive incorrect speculative precharges by left-shifting the base step. Let $n$ denote the number of consecutive incorrect speculative precharges observed so far, capped at $N_\mathrm{max} = 7$. The effective step size is
\begin{equation}
s = s_0 \cdot 2^{\min(n,\;N_\mathrm{max})}
\end{equation}
where $s_0 = \texttt{BASE\_STEP} = 50$~cycles. A conflict resets the counter to zero. A correct speculative precharge decrements the counter by one (the SD refinement in Section~\ref{sec:refinements}), dampening future backoff without fully erasing it. This mechanism enables rapid convergence during sustained high-locality phases. The timeout can reach the upper bound within a small number of feedback events.

\paragraph{(c) Continuous adjustment range.}
The timeout adjusts within $[T_\mathrm{MIN}, T_\mathrm{MAX}] = [50, 3200]$~cycles. $T_\mathrm{MIN}$ matches \texttt{BASE\_STEP} to prevent degeneration to close-page behavior; $T_\mathrm{MAX}$ prevents indefinitely open rows. The exponential backoff traverses the full range in as few as six escalation events.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:craft} presents the complete pseudocode, including the three refinements described in Section~\ref{sec:refinements} (annotated [RS], [RW], [SD]). Fig.~\ref{fig:feedback} illustrates the overall structure.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../figures/output/feedback_loop.pdf}
\caption{CRAFT timeout-control logic. Timeout expiration triggers speculative precharge, after which the next access determines a wrong or right precharge outcome. A new request that arrives before timeout expiration is instead either a row hit or a conflict. Wrong precharges incur $t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD}$ in avoidable overhead and escalate the timeout with exponential backoff. Conflicts incur only $t_\mathrm{RP}$ in missed overlap opportunity and reduce the timeout with a cost-proportional decrement. \begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{CRAFT Feedback Loop}
\label{alg:craft}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{On each incoming bank request (while row open):}
\If{$\mathit{new\_row} \neq \mathit{open\_row}$} \Comment{Conflict}
  \State $\mathit{step} \leftarrow \texttt{BASE\_STEP} \times t_\mathrm{RP} / (t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD})$
  \State \textbf{if} $\mathit{is\_read}$: $\mathit{step} \leftarrow 2 \times \mathit{step}$ \Comment{[RW]}
  \State $\mathit{timeout} \leftarrow \mathit{timeout} - \mathit{step}$ \Comment{Reduce timeout}
  \State $\mathit{reopen\_streak} \leftarrow 0$;\; $\mathit{right\_streak} \leftarrow 0$
  \State $\mathit{timeout} \leftarrow \mathrm{clamp}(\mathit{timeout},\;T_\mathrm{MIN},\;T_\mathrm{MAX})$
\EndIf
\Statex
\State \textbf{On each bank \textsc{Act} event (following timeout precharge):}
\If{$\mathit{prev\_closed\_by\_timeout}$}
  \If{$\mathit{new\_row} = \mathit{prev\_row}$} \Comment{Incorrect speculative precharge}
    \State $\mathit{step} \leftarrow \texttt{BASE\_STEP} \ll \min(\mathit{reopen\_streak},\;\texttt{SHIFT\_CAP})$
    \State \textbf{if} $\mathit{is\_write}$: $\mathit{step} \leftarrow \mathit{step} \gg 1$ \Comment{[RW]}
    \State $\mathit{timeout} \leftarrow \mathit{timeout} + \mathit{step}$ \Comment{Increase timeout}
    \State $\mathit{reopen\_streak} \leftarrow \min(\mathit{reopen\_streak} + 1,\;7)$
    \State $\mathit{right\_streak} \leftarrow 0$
  \Else \Comment{Correct speculative precharge}
    \State $\mathit{reopen\_streak} \leftarrow \max(\mathit{reopen\_streak} - 1,\;0)$ \Comment{[SD]}
    \State $\mathit{right\_streak} \leftarrow \min(\mathit{right\_streak} + 1,\;7)$ \Comment{[RS]}
    \If{$\mathit{right\_streak} \geq 4$} \Comment{[RS]}
      \State $\mathit{timeout} \leftarrow \mathit{timeout} - \texttt{BASE\_STEP} \times t_\mathrm{RP} / (2\,(t_\mathrm{RP} + t_\mathrm{RCD}))$ \Comment{[RS]}
    \EndIf
  \EndIf
  \State $\mathit{timeout} \leftarrow \mathrm{clamp}(\mathit{timeout},\;T_\mathrm{MIN},\;T_\mathrm{MAX})$
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Precharge-Path Refinements}
\label{sec:refinements}

The core feedback loop provides the primary adaptive capability.
The following three refinements exploit additional information available from precharge outcomes to improve adaptation precision.
All three are lightweight and introduce no additional hardware structures beyond simple counters.

\paragraph{(a) Timeout Reduction After Consecutive Correct Speculative Precharges (RS).}
After four or more consecutive correct speculative precharges, the timeout decrements by half the conflict decrement.
This gradual reduction prevents timeout values from remaining at elevated levels after a high-locality phase has ended.
Without RS, a bank that transitions from a high-locality phase to a low-locality phase would maintain an unnecessarily large timeout until a conflict explicitly triggers a timeout reduction.
RS provides a proactive path for timeout reduction in the absence of explicit conflict signals.
RS adds three bits per bank for the $\mathit{right\_streak}$ counter.

\paragraph{(b) Read/Write Cost Differentiation (RW).}
An incorrect speculative precharge blocking a read stalls the processor pipeline, while one blocking a write is absorbed by the write buffer in the memory controller. CRAFT exploits this asymmetry by halving the increment for write incorrect speculative precharges and doubling the decrement for read conflicts. RW requires no additional storage.

\paragraph{(c) Streak Decay (SD).}
Each correct speculative precharge decrements $\mathit{reopen\_streak}$ by one, preventing a historically elevated streak from causing disproportionately large increments later. SD requires no additional storage.

Section~\ref{sec:ablation} validates the effectiveness of these three refinements through an ablation study.

\subsection{Hardware Overhead}
\label{sec:hardware}

Each bank maintains five fields totaling 35~bits: a 12-bit timeout value, two 3-bit streak counters, a 16-bit previous-row address, and a 1-bit timeout-precharge flag. For 32 banks per channel, this amounts to 140~bytes per channel. CRAFT requires no specialized hardware structures; the critical path requires only one comparison and one addition or subtraction.

\subsection{Workloads}

We select 12 memory-intensive workloads from three benchmark suites. Graph workloads are drawn from LIGRA~\cite{Shun13} and CRONO~\cite{Ahmad15} using three SNAP graphs~\cite{Leskovec16}: roadNet-CA, higgs, and soc-pokec. Scientific computing workloads are sphinx3 and wrf from SPEC CPU2006~\cite{SPEC06}. Representative phases are identified using SimPoint~\cite{Sherwood02} with maxK~=~30 and 100M-instruction intervals; each simulation point uses 20M instructions for warmup and 80M instructions for measurement. The suite is intentionally biased toward memory-intensive single-core behavior, so we interpret the results within that target setting.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines_meth}

We compare CRAFT against ABP~\cite{Awasthi11}, DYMPL~\cite{Rafique22}, and INTAP~\cite{Intel06}. All baselines are implemented in the same infrastructure under the DDR5-4800 configuration in Table~\ref{tab:simconfig}. The controller scheduling policy, address mapping, queue capacities, and trace inputs remain fixed across schemes; only the row buffer management logic differs. Unless otherwise noted, the CRAFT configuration evaluated in Sections~\ref{sec:ipc}--\ref{sec:timeout_dist} includes the core feedback loop and all three precharge-outcome-based refinements (RS, RW, SD) described in Section~\ref{sec:refinements}. Section~\ref{sec:ablation} isolates the contribution of each component.

\subsection{Metrics}

We report three metrics. \textbf{IPC} is the primary performance metric; we report per-workload improvement relative to each baseline. \textbf{Read row buffer hit rate} measures the fraction of read requests served from the currently activated row; write hit rates are excluded because writes complete in the memory controller's write buffer. \textbf{Average read latency} is the mean DRAM service time for read requests in DRAM cycles.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Static page policies set a fixed open or closed behavior for all accesses. JEDEC~\cite{DDR345} defines per-command auto-precharge, and Minimalist Open-page~\cite{Kaseridis11} closes a row after a fixed hit count. Predictor-based schemes improve on these by inferring per-row reuse from access history or address signals~\cite{Awasthi11,Ghasempour16}; their storage cost scales with the number of tracked rows. Adaptive schemes reduce this overhead by operating at the bank granularity. INTAP~\cite{Intel06} adjusts a per-bank timeout through a fixed-step mistake counter but applies identical adjustments to both failure modes of speculative precharge. DYMPL~\cite{Rafique22} applies a perceptron over indirect features, and~\cite{Ipek08} uses reinforcement learning. Both require auxiliary model state and do not directly observe precharge outcome cost. CRAFT distinguishes the two failure modes by cost and uses this asymmetry to set adjustment magnitudes without additional model state.

A substantial body of work addresses DRAM performance from orthogonal angles. Scheduling algorithms such as FR-FCFS~\cite{Rixner00}, stall-time fair scheduling~\cite{Mutlu07}, Thread Cluster Memory Scheduling~\cite{Kim10}, and Staged Memory Scheduling~\cite{Ausavarungnirun12} govern request issue order within the command queue; the virtual write queue~\cite{Stuecheli10} coordinates write-back policy with row management. Multi-core interference proposals~\cite{Muralidhara11,Subramanian13} partition bandwidth or estimate slowdown to improve fairness across applications. At the device level, SALP~\cite{Kim12} and Tiered-Latency DRAM~\cite{Lee13} reduce access overhead through bank and subarray organization, RAIDR~\cite{Liu12} reduces refresh frequency through retention-aware scheduling, and Udipi et al.~\cite{Udipi10} propose energy-aware rank arrangements. These directions are complementary to CRAFT, which operates within the memory controller policy layer and requires no changes to the DRAM device or scheduler.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents CRAFT, a lightweight row buffer management scheme that exploits the cost asymmetry between the two failure modes of speculative precharge. CRAFT combines asymmetric timeout updates with exponential backoff and requires only 140~bytes of storage per channel.

Across 12 memory-intensive workloads, CRAFT improves IPC by 7.73\%, 3.10\%, and 2.84\% over ABP, DYMPL, and INTAP, respectively. CRAFT also improves the average read row buffer hit rate by 5.62\% over the best baseline and reduces average read latency by 2.74\%. Our ablation study shows that precharge-outcome-based refinements improve adaptation precision, while additional conflict-based heuristics do not help in this setting.

These results support CRAFT as a practical option for the evaluated single-core DDR5 configuration. The current evaluation emphasizes memory-intensive single-core workloads, and broader multi-core and cross-standard validation remains future work.
