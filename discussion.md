## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Why Direct Feedback Suffices

A natural question is why a simple feedback loop based on precharge outcomes achieves superior performance compared to schemes that employ richer feature sets or learned models. We draw an analogy to classical control theory: CRAFT operates as a proportional controller with cost-weighted error terms, where the precharge outcome (right, wrong, or conflict) serves as the error signal and the timeout value as the control variable. In contrast, learning-based approaches such as DYMPL attempt model-predictive control by estimating the optimal policy from multiple indirect features—row access frequency, inter-access interval, bank utilization, and others. While model-predictive control can, in principle, anticipate future states, it introduces estimation error and training latency that are difficult to eliminate in practice.

The key insight underlying CRAFT's effectiveness is that the precharge outcome already encodes the relevant information about whether the current timeout is appropriate. A wrong precharge directly indicates that the timeout was too short; a conflict indicates that it was too long; a right precharge confirms that the current value is suitable. This direct observation eliminates the need for intermediate feature extraction and inference. The ablation study in Section 5.4 provides empirical support for this argument: augmenting the core feedback loop with conflict-path signals (phase reset, queue-depth scaling) degraded performance, indicating that the three-category precharge outcome already captures sufficient feedback and additional signal sources introduce interference rather than complementary information.

### 6.2 Limitations

CRAFT's adaptive mechanism relies on the existence of exploitable temporal locality variation across execution phases. In workloads where row buffer behavior is entirely uniform, the feedback loop converges but provides no advantage over a well-chosen static policy. Specifically, under purely random access patterns where row buffer hit rates fall below one percent, any timeout-based policy converges to behavior equivalent to the closed-page policy, and CRAFT offers no improvement over a static closed-page configuration. Symmetrically, under purely sequential access patterns where nearly every access hits in the row buffer, any timeout-based policy converges to open-page behavior, and CRAFT provides no benefit over a static open-page configuration. In both extremes, the timeout value becomes irrelevant because the access pattern admits no decision boundary between premature and delayed precharge.

Our evaluation focuses on single-core workloads. Although the per-bank state organization of CRAFT—with no shared per-channel structures—suggests favorable multi-core scalability, we have not validated this experimentally. In multi-core environments, inter-thread interference at shared banks may alter the feedback dynamics: precharge outcomes would reflect the combined access pattern of multiple threads rather than a single coherent stream. Investigating CRAFT's behavior under multi-programmed and multi-threaded workloads is an important direction for future work.

### 6.3 Orthogonality with Other Microarchitectural Techniques

CRAFT operates entirely within the memory controller and makes decisions based solely on per-bank precharge outcomes. It does not inspect or modify the behavior of any upstream or downstream microarchitectural component. Consequently, CRAFT is orthogonal to hardware prefetchers, cache replacement policies, and memory request schedulers. A prefetcher alters the stream of requests arriving at the memory controller, which in turn changes the distribution of precharge outcomes; CRAFT's feedback loop would naturally adapt to this altered distribution without requiring any modification. Similarly, changes to cache replacement or scheduling priority would shift the balance among right, wrong, and conflict outcomes, and CRAFT would respond accordingly through its existing adjustment mechanism.

This orthogonality also extends to storage compression techniques for row buffer management state. For example, HAPPY [Ghasempour+16] reduces predictor storage from per-row to per-address-bit granularity. Because CRAFT's per-bank state is already minimal at 35 bits per bank, the storage compression benefit of such techniques is limited in CRAFT's context, but the two approaches address complementary aspects of the design space and could be combined in settings that require both prediction-based and timeout-based row buffer management.
