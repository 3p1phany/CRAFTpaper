## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Why Direct Feedback Suffices

A natural question is why a simple feedback loop based on precharge outcomes achieves superior performance compared to schemes with richer feature sets or learned models. We draw an analogy to classical control theory. CRAFT operates as a proportional controller with cost-weighted error terms. The three precharge outcomes, namely right, wrong, and conflict, serve as the error signal. The timeout value serves as the control variable. In contrast, learning-based approaches such as DYMPL attempt model-predictive control by estimating the optimal policy from multiple indirect features. These features include row access frequency, inter-access interval, bank utilization, and others. Model-predictive control can, in principle, anticipate future states. However, it introduces estimation error and training latency. These overheads are difficult to eliminate in practice.

CRAFT's effectiveness rests on a key insight. The precharge outcome already encodes the relevant information about whether the current timeout is appropriate. A wrong precharge directly indicates that the timeout was too short. A conflict indicates that it was too long. A right precharge confirms that the current value is suitable. This direct observation eliminates the need for intermediate feature extraction and inference. The ablation study in Section 5.4 provides empirical support for this argument. Augmenting the core feedback loop with conflict-path signals such as phase reset and queue-depth scaling degraded performance. This result confirms that the three-category precharge outcome already captures sufficient feedback. Additional signal sources introduce interference rather than complementary information.

A related question concerns the distinction between CRAFT and INTAP. INTAP adopts a similar feedback-based timeout adjustment mechanism (Section 7) and shares CRAFT's principle of direct observation over indirect inference. The key distinction is the treatment of error asymmetry. The two types of precharge outcome impose fundamentally different penalties. A premature precharge forces a redundant reactivation on the next access to the same row. An excessively delayed precharge stalls the incoming request by the full precharge overhead. INTAP disregards this cost difference and applies uniform adjustment magnitudes. CRAFT derives its step sizes directly from DRAM timing parameters. This cost-weighted feedback enables faster convergence to appropriate timeout values and more accurate tracking of phase transitions. The consistent performance advantage of CRAFT over INTAP in Section 5 confirms the importance of cost-asymmetric feedback treatment.

### 6.2 Limitations

CRAFT's adaptive mechanism relies on the existence of exploitable temporal locality variation across execution phases. For workloads with entirely uniform row buffer behavior, the feedback loop converges but provides no advantage over a well-chosen static policy. Specifically, under purely random access patterns with row buffer hit rates below one percent, any timeout-based policy converges to behavior equivalent to the closed-page policy. CRAFT offers no improvement over a static closed-page configuration in this case. Symmetrically, under purely sequential access patterns with near-unity row buffer hit rates, any timeout-based policy converges to open-page behavior. CRAFT provides no benefit over a static open-page configuration. In both extremes, the timeout value becomes irrelevant. The access pattern admits no decision boundary between premature and delayed precharge.

Our evaluation focuses on single-core workloads. The per-bank state organization of CRAFT, with no shared per-channel structures, suggests favorable multi-core scalability. However, we have not validated this experimentally. In multi-core environments, inter-thread interference at shared banks may alter the feedback dynamics. Precharge outcomes would reflect the combined access pattern of multiple threads rather than a single coherent stream. Investigating CRAFT's behavior under multi-programmed and multi-threaded workloads is an important direction for future work.
